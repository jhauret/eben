EBEN: Extreme Bandwidth Extension Network
applied to speech signals captured with noise-resilient microphones

Submitted to ICASSP 2023

Julien Hauret, Thomas Joubaud , Véronique Zimpfer, Éric Bavu

Paper Notebook Code Prototype

Abstract

We present Extreme Bandwidth Extension Network (EBEN), a generative adversarial network (GAN) that enhances audio measured with noise-resilient microphones. This type of capture equipment suppresses ambient noise at the expense of speech bandwidth, thereby requiring signal enhancement techniques to recover the wideband speech signal. EBEN leverages a multiband decomposition of the raw captured speech to decrease the data time-domain dimensions, and give better control over the full-band signal. This multiband representation is fed to a U-Net-like model, which adopts a combination of feature and adversarial losses to recover an enhanced audio signal. We also benefit from this original representation in the proposed discriminator architecture. Our approach can achieve state-of-the-art results with a lightweight generator and real-time compatible operation.

Funny experiments

In this example, we trained a bandwidth extension model with on a given language dataset, and inferred it on a degraded audio in another language. In the obtained enhanced audio, you'll notice a strong "accent", based on the language the model was trained for. Objective metrics do not penalize this behavior and give similar scores to models trained in the same language as inference.

Perceptual Evaluation

In order to evaluate the performance of the trained models, we performed two MUSHRA listening tests with 88 (resp. 82) participants for each test : Perceptual sound quality and speech intelligibility. Each participant had to listen to 7 different experiments (7 different speakers). For each experiment, the MUSHRA procedure consisted in giving a note between 0 and 100 for one of the two attributes.
